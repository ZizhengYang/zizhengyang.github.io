Advantages and disadvantages of the Mapping Segregation DC project

Mapping Segregation DC is a project that records the history of housing segregation in Washington, DC. Housing segregation is a long-standing act of depriving minority groups of equal access to housing in American history. Of course, the way it is implemented in different periods is different. Sometimes represented as service providers refuse to provide specific real estate for minority groups, and sometimes represented as published illegal measures or misinformation to mislead minority groups. The historical story that took place in Washington DC is a microcosm of Mapping Segregation. In order to discover the far-reaching role of this Mapping Segregation in the history of American architecture, it was led by the Historic Preservation Fund and other institutions, with the famous scholars Mara Cherkasky and​​ Sarah Jane Shoenfel and others led the establishment of the Mapping Segregation DC project and built a website for people to retrieve the information collected by the project.

When people enter the website, the first thing they see is a set of carousel images, which occupies almost the entire page. These extremely contemporary pictures instantly bring readers back to those times. If a person enters Mapping Segregation DC while searching for information, carousel images will arouse his or her interest. However, this highly artistic design is not necessary for an architectural history database, and it may even have the opposite effect. To give an example, add a reader who is interested in a certain historical event mentioned in carousel images and comments and wants to conduct a deeper research, but he or she will find that there is nothing more than pictures and simple descriptions. For many links, I can only put aside the question temporarily and continue to read down.

In the part of the “Story map”, Mapping Segregation DC uses Arcgis' multimedia technology to provide readers with a perfect experience. If you really want to understand the development of architectural history, then displaying city-related pictures is an intuitive way. You only need to slide the article on the left, and the corresponding multimedia file will be dynamically rendered on the right: it can be historical photos, document screenshots, or dynamic ethnic distribution maps made by arcgis, income distribution maps, maps of real estate agents, and so on. When a person tries to understand the knowledge about Mapping Segregation, he or she can easily get rich and convenient academic resources, which is a very useful design. Here I want to take the FHA insured housing and the visualization of the history of racial covenant as examples to illustrate this convenience.

FHA house insurance can be said to be the beginning of the entire housing isolation system. As a database, it is necessary to help readers understand this important historical event. In FHA insured housing, a dynamic FHA community grading map has been added to the website. The entire Washington is divided into green areas and red areas. The former includes the A-E grading community, and the latter includes only the lowest rated F, G, and H. People usually think that the various ratings of FHA house grading are classified based on future development and community safety, while black areas and other colored areas are classified as low ratings for reasons of racial discrimination. In fact, the low-grading G grading is just to mark the humble neighborhoods designed for white people with poor development prospects. This means that community grading is much more complicated than what people usually do. The difference between ratings is not solely dependent on house insurance, but a comprehensive classification. This does not mean that the racial discrimination of red lining has diminished. On the contrary, in the F district rating document, the FHA claimed that it was the immigration of black residents that caused these neighborhoods to quickly slip into slums. Even the H zone rating is a real estate developed specifically for blacks, which is already blatantly racially discriminatory. There is another interesting thing to note about this map. The gradient of each community rating is very large. More generally speaking, neighborhoods with extremely high scores are often very close to neighborhoods with extremely low scores. If you think of it being separated by a wall The living conditions are so different, which is really incredible. Overall, the Mapping Segregation DC project is very successful, using modern technology to help people better learn and understand the history of redlining in architecture.

Another graph that may also be very representative is the visualization of the history of racial covenant. The chapter list of this article is clearer and comes in chronological order. There are many interesting points in this. For instance, it can be noted that in the early days (1940s), the main gathering areas of people of color were the areas in the city center except for government buildings. This corresponds to the history of the re-use of the old city by the rich and poor at that time. What's more interesting is that from the 1970s to the 1980s, the neighborhood to the north of the White House suddenly changed from a predominantly non-white to a predominantly white one. Residents who moved to western communities in the past 30 years have moved back. This is because black homeowners moved to Prince George’s County for children’s education, and investors and young professionals bought their houses.

All in all, the Mapping Segregation DC project has done a very good job in presenting its content. But sometimes it is too much about artistic effects rather than academic experience. It would be very helpful if you can provide more convenient links and more clear mind maps.
